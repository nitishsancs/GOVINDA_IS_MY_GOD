### Theme 1: The regulatory scope and conceptual foundation of liquidity risk under the ALM Directions

**1. Question:** Explain the regulatory scope and conceptual foundation of liquidity risk under the ALM Directions.

**Answer:** The ALM Directions apply to commercial banks from the date of issue and frame liquidity as the ability to fund asset growth and meet expected and unexpected cash and collateral obligations at reasonable cost without unacceptable loss. They distinguish funding liquidity risk, market liquidity risk, intraday liquidity, and interest rate risk so that the rest of the framework rests on a common prudential vocabulary. This foundation matters because liquidity problems can spill beyond a single institution and become systemic. Clear definitions make later governance, measurement, limits, and reporting internally consistent.

### Theme 2: The Board’s responsibilities for liquidity risk and intraday oversight

**6. Question:** Explain the Board’s responsibilities for liquidity risk and intraday oversight.

**Answer:** The Board is responsible for overall liquidity-risk management and must decide the bank’s strategy, policies, procedures, and risk tolerance. It must understand the liquidity profile of branches, subsidiaries, and associates, approve policies on intraday sources and business-line tolerances, and ensure management has authority to identify, measure, monitor, and manage liquidity risk. This is critical because liquidity stress can emerge faster than many other risks, so risk appetite and escalation authority cannot be left vague or informal. Board oversight aligns tolerance with the bank’s funding capacity and financial condition.

### Theme 3: The distinct roles of the RMC and ALCO in liquidity governance

**11. Question:** Explain the distinct roles of the RMC and ALCO in liquidity governance.

**Answer:** The RMC evaluates liquidity risk and its interaction with credit, market, and operational risk, while ALCO implements the Board’s liquidity strategy and enforces adherence to risk tolerance. ALCO decides maturity profile, funding mix, operational structure, assumptions, stress-test use, transfer pricing, and regular reporting to the Board or RMC. The separation matters because one body evaluates and challenges risk, while the other turns policy into actual funding, pricing, and balance-sheet decisions. That design reduces the chance that liquidity strategy becomes detached from day-to-day treasury reality.

*Grounding:* 163MD.pdf, Chapter III, paras 23-27.

### Theme 6: The flow approach and the use of the Structural Liquidity Statement

**26. Question:** Explain the flow approach and the use of the Structural Liquidity Statement.

**Answer:** Under the flow approach, liquidity is measured by tracking cash inflows and outflows in time buckets through the Structural Liquidity Statement. Banks must prepare domestic SLS daily, use prescribed parts for domestic currency, foreign currency, combined Indian operations, overseas branch operations, and consolidated operations, and report them to RBI at the required periodicities. This is important because cash-flow mismatches are often the earliest warning signal of future funding stress. A bank can look comfortable in aggregate while still facing sharp short-bucket deficits.

### Theme 7: Behavioural analysis, assumptions, and variance testing in liquidity measurement

**31. Question:** Explain behavioural analysis, assumptions, and variance testing in liquidity measurement.

**Answer:** The Directions require behavioural analysis of assets, liabilities, and off-balance-sheet items using assumptions, trend analysis, and time-series evidence. Banks must capture rollover behaviour, undated products, prepayments, premature closures, and option exercise, and validate assumptions through variance analysis at least once every six months. This matters because contractual maturity often diverges from actual cash behaviour. If assumptions are weak, liquidity projections can look precise while still being unreliable.

### Theme 8: The stock approach, structural liquidity ratios, and internal prudential limits

**36. Question:** Explain the stock approach, structural liquidity ratios, and internal prudential limits.

**Answer:** The stock approach complements cash-flow analysis through ratios such as core deposits to total assets, temporary assets to volatile liabilities, and measures of illiquid assets funded by core deposits. Banks may choose the ratios they use, but internal limits for them must be Board-approved and aligned with the bank’s capabilities, experience, and risk profile. These ratios are important because they reveal structural dependence on volatile liabilities or weak liquidity buffers even when near-term flows seem manageable. They provide a balance-sheet perspective that the flow approach alone cannot give.

### Theme 9: Short-term mismatch monitoring and liquidity management across currencies

**41. Question:** Explain short-term mismatch monitoring and liquidity management across currencies.

**Answer:** The main regulatory focus is on short-term mismatches up to 30 days, with cumulative negative mismatch caps of 5, 10, 15, and 20 percent for the next-day, 2-7 day, 8-14 day, and 15-30 day buckets. Banks must also maintain systems for major-currency liquidity, considering foreign funding access, backup facilities, transferability across entities, and convertibility risk. This matters because liquidity crises often crystallise in very short horizons and in specific currencies rather than across the balance sheet uniformly. Currency blind spots can make an aggregate position misleading.

### Theme 11: Host-home rule differences and cross-border operations in ALM and KYC

**251. Question:** Explain host-home rule differences and cross-border operations in ALM and KYC.

**Answer:** In ALM, overseas branches and subsidiaries must manage liquidity according to the more stringent of home or host rules, subject to entity type and realistic support assumptions. ### Theme 20: An integrated crisis in which liquidity strain, payment disruption, and suspicious transactions appear together

**296. Question:** Explain an integrated crisis in which liquidity strain, payment disruption, and suspicious transactions appear together.

**Answer:** In a realistic banking crisis, market stress may tighten funding, disrupt intraday settlement, and coincide with unusual customer activity, correspondent-bank concern, or sanctions-screening matches. The two RBI directions together require the bank to respond through Board oversight, MIS, escalation, stress analysis, contingency planning, customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and timely external reporting. This integrated perspective matters because real crises do not arrive in tidy regulatory compartments. A bank that manages liquidity and AML/CFT separately may react too slowly when the same event affects both domains at once.
